Overview

The biggest event
in the season.

The Haunting is Call of Duty's annual Halloween seasonal event — a high-visibility, multi-week live-service experience deployed across Modern Warfare II and Warzone 2.0. In 2023, I led UX for the event as the primary designer responsible for shaping how millions of players would discover, navigate, and engage with its content.

The event included limited-time modes, exclusive operator bundles, seasonal challenges, cosmetic rewards, and a dedicated event hub — all of which needed to be surfaced clearly across a complex existing UI ecosystem.

"How do you design a seasonal experience that feels exciting and discoverable — without overwhelming a player base already navigating a deep, content-rich game?"

The Challenge

Visibility vs. noise.

Live-service events are a UX paradox. You need players to feel the excitement and urgency of limited-time content — but the game's existing UI systems are already dense. The Haunting had to land with impact without creating confusion, frustration, or friction.

Key tensions we had to resolve:

01
Content surfacing at scale
Dozens of challenges, rewards, limited-time modes, and bundles needed to be organized, discoverable, and prioritized without overwhelming the main interface.
02
Cross-title consistency
The event spanned both MW II and Warzone 2.0, requiring UX patterns that worked across different game contexts and UI systems.
03
Motivating long-term engagement
Seasonal events live or die on whether players keep coming back. The progression and reward structure had to feel meaningful and achievable over multiple weeks.
04
Multi-team coordination
I was designing in parallel with product, engineering, art, and narrative teams — all operating on tight live-service timelines.
My Role

End-to-end UX ownership.

I served as the primary UX designer for The Haunting event across its full lifecycle — from early strategy and concepting through final delivery and QA. My responsibilities spanned interaction design, information architecture, user flow definition, and cross-functional collaboration.

🗺️
Experience Strategy
Defined the overall UX approach for the event hub, progression structure, and content surfacing strategy.
⚙️
Systems Design
Designed the challenge and reward systems, ensuring clarity at every step of the completion flow.
🔗
Cross-Functional Collab
Worked closely with product, art, engineering, and narrative to align design decisions with technical and creative constraints.
📐
High-Fidelity Prototyping
Produced all UX deliverables in Figma — wireframes, annotated flows, and production-ready UI specs.
🔄
Interaction Design
Defined transitions, state changes, and interaction patterns that balanced clarity with the event's cinematic tone.
📦
Shipped Delivery
Supported production all the way through ship, working with engineering to ensure implementation fidelity.
UX Strategy

Build a hub. Reduce friction. Drive return visits.

The core UX strategy was to create a centralized event hub that served as the single source of truth for The Haunting — a space players could return to daily to understand where they stood, what to do next, and what was waiting for them.

Rather than scattering event content across the existing menus, we consolidated the experience into a dedicated event layer — accessible from the main navigation — that gave the event its own visual identity while integrating cleanly into the game's existing UI ecosystem.

"Players shouldn't have to hunt for the event. The event should feel like the place everyone is going — obvious, exciting, and rewarding to return to."

Design Principles for the Event

→
Instant legibility
Any player should be able to open the event hub and immediately understand their progress, active challenges, and next unlock — within 5 seconds.
→
Layered depth
Casual players see the surface: featured content and clear next steps. Engaged players can dig deeper into challenge trees, reward paths, and event lore.
→
Atmospheric integration
The UI should feel like part of the event, not a layer on top of it. Visual hierarchy and motion work with the Halloween aesthetic, not against it.
Key Features

Systems that keep players coming back.

The Haunting hub was built around four core UX systems, each designed to work together as a cohesive progression experience.

🏚️
Event Hub
Centralized landing space for all event content — progress, featured items, and challenge overview in one glanceable view.
📋
Challenge System
Tiered daily and weekly challenges with clear completion states, tracking, and unlock visibility for sustained engagement.
🎖️
Reward Path
A visible, satisfying reward progression that communicated value at each tier — driving completion without artificial difficulty.
🕹️
Mode Integration
Seamless surfacing of limited-time modes directly within the event context, reducing discovery friction for new game experiences.
👻
Operator Showcase
A curated view of event-exclusive operators and bundles, presented in context of the seasonal narrative.
🔔
Smart Notifications
Contextual callouts and nudges within the UI that directed players toward unfinished challenges and expiring content.
Outcomes

Shipped. Measured. Meaningful.

The Haunting was one of the highest-performing seasonal events in Call of Duty history. The UX improvements contributed directly to measurable player engagement and retention gains.

25M+
Players reached by the live event
+15%
Seasonal event participation growth

Beyond the numbers, The Haunting set a new internal benchmark for how Infinity Ward approaches seasonal event UX — with centralized hubs, clear challenge hierarchy, and tighter integration between event content and core navigation.

Reflection

What I'd take forward.

The Haunting reinforced something I believe strongly: the best UX decisions in complex systems come from deeply understanding what the user actually needs to feel and do — not just what the product needs to show. In a content-heavy live-service game, that clarity is everything.

Working at this scale also sharpened my ability to move fast without sacrificing quality — balancing velocity with craft in a high-stakes, high-visibility production environment. Those skills transfer directly to any fast-moving product team.

Overview

Performance tracking
built for the mission.

Mission Ready started with a personal problem. As a Marine Corps veteran, I knew firsthand how stressful PFT and CFT prep could be — and how inadequate the existing tools were. Most Marines relied on spreadsheets, generic fitness apps, or their own mental math to track readiness.

I designed Mission Ready as the app I wished I had — a focused, mobile-first performance tool that handles PFT and CFT score calculation, structures training programs around real Marine fitness standards, and keeps users motivated with clear progress tracking.

"Marines are goal-oriented. The app should match that mentality — no fluff, just clarity on where you stand, what you need to do, and how close you are to mission-ready."

The Problem

No tool built for this user.

U.S. Marines face biannual physical fitness tests — the PFT (Physical Fitness Test) and CFT (Combat Fitness Test) — with scoring tables that vary by age and gender. Getting an accurate score requires cross-referencing tables, performing manual calculations, and understanding tier thresholds. The cognitive overhead is significant.

01
Calculation complexity
Scoring tables are multi-variable (age group, gender, exercise type, rep count, time) — making mental math error-prone and frustrating.
02
No structured training path
Existing fitness apps aren't designed around Marine standards. There's no structured "path to first class" for PFT or CFT readiness.
03
Motivation and accountability
Without visibility into progress over time, training motivation drops — especially for Marines between duty cycles.
04
Speed in context
Marines are busy. The app needs to deliver value in seconds — not require extensive setup or constant manual input.
Product Goals

Four goals. One focused product.

🎯
Instant Score Clarity
Any Marine should be able to input their performance numbers and get an accurate, classified score in under 30 seconds.
📈
Structured Progression
A daily and weekly training program specifically designed to move users from their current level toward first-class fitness standards.
📊
Visible Progress
Charts, logs, and deltas that make improvement visible — keeping users motivated over weeks and months of preparation.
⚡
Designed for Speed
A streamlined, fast-loading UI that respects user time — built for quick daily check-ins, not lengthy sessions.
Core Features

Built around what Marines actually need.

01
PFT & CFT Score Calculator
Input your reps, time, and age group — get an accurate score, your class tier (1st, 2nd, 3rd), and points breakdown instantly. Built on official Marine Corps scoring tables.
02
Training Programs
Structured 30-day training programs designed for PFT and CFT prep — with daily workouts, progression tracking, and rest day logic built in.
03
Progress Dashboard
A clean, data-forward home screen that shows current score estimate, training streak, weekly goal completion, and trend over time.
04
Workout Log
Log each session's performance numbers with a minimal input flow — minimal friction, maximum data capture, with automatic score calculation post-log.
05
Standards Reference
In-app access to official Marine Corps PFT and CFT scoring standards — organized by age group and test type, always available offline.
UX Decisions

Intentional choices for performance-minded users.

Every design decision in Mission Ready was filtered through one lens: does this make a Marine's prep faster, clearer, or more motivating? Here are the key UX calls that shaped the product.

"Marines are trained to assess, decide, and act — fast. The UX should match that operating tempo. No buried features. No cognitive overhead."

🔢
Score-First Hierarchy
The score is always the dominant element on every relevant screen — large, clear, and immediately readable. Supporting data is secondary.
🔍
Persistent Score Context
Class tier (1st, 2nd, 3rd Class) is always shown alongside the score — because Marines think in tiers, not raw numbers.
✏️
Minimal Input Flow
Calculator flows are designed for single-thumb use with large tap targets, numeric pickers, and a maximum of 3 inputs per calculation.
🌑
Dark-First Design
A dark, high-contrast UI optimized for outdoor use — important for a user base that trains outside in varying light conditions.
📉
Delta-Forward Progress
Progress is always shown as change over time — "+8 points from last session" is more motivating than a static number.
⚙️
Offline-Ready
Core features — score calculation, standards reference, active workout — function fully without a network connection.
Impact

Designed for results, built to scale.

Mission Ready was designed with a clear user value proposition from the start: save time, eliminate confusion, and make fitness readiness feel achievable. The expected product outcomes reflect this focus.

Reflection

Design from lived experience.

Mission Ready is the project that most directly connects my two identities: Marine and designer. Being the user gave me an unfair advantage in understanding what actually matters — and what doesn't. There's no substitute for that level of domain knowledge when designing a focused utility product.

The broader lesson I take from this project applies to any product work: the best tools are invisible. When a Marine opens Mission Ready, they shouldn't be thinking about the app — they should be thinking about their score, their next session, and their goal. That's what good product UX looks like.

Design Philosophy
"Good design is
invisible to the
user who needs it."

I believe the mark of excellent UX is when a user accomplishes their goal without ever thinking about the interface. That's the bar I design to. Not delight for its own sake, not visual novelty for its own sake — but clarity, usability, and outcomes that feel effortless.

I think about design from a systems level first. Before I touch a component, I want to understand the full experience — where users come from, what they're trying to do, what happens when things go wrong. Strong interaction design comes from strong information architecture comes from strong product thinking.

And I believe in shipping. Beautiful work that never makes it to users isn't design — it's art. I care about velocity, production fidelity, and working closely with engineers to make sure what we build actually matches what we designed.
